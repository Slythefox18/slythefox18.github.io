Background Information


Garlic Mustard was brought to Canada as an edible plant in the early 1800s (1) This was because the plant is high in vitamin A and vitamin C and smells similar to garlic (1) As an example, the plant contains more vitamin C per weight than oragnes (2) Since then, the plant has spread accross much of North America



Impacts


Chemicals in the roots of Garlic Mustard have been shown to prevent growth of other plants (3) The plant's chemicals also prevent the growth of arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF), a fungi that helps other trees and plants absorb nutrients and water (3) The loss of AMF allows non-native plants to displace native plants altering the ecosystem (3) This effect can last for years after the Garlic Mustard has been removed (3)



Species Control


Mangement

Hand Pulling: You can hand pull Garlic Mustard out of the ground (1). You must make sure to remove the entire root of the plant to avoid the plant resprouting (3). To be most effective, it is ideal to replant native species in place of the Garlic Mustard (3).


Mowing: You can cut 2nd year plants at the base of the stem, ideally just after flowering, and before the plants produce seeds (3). This may need to be repeated since the plants may flower at diffrent times. It is better than hand-pulling since it causes less soil disturbance (3).


Disposal

Garlic Mustard should not be composted (3). Pulled Plants are still able to produce seeds (3). Any plant material should be put in garbage bags or yard waste bags (3). After this they should be left in direct sunlight for a week, this will kill any plant material. After this, bags should be dried and burned or sent to landfill (3).
